# 2. MG5 — CASE SUMMARY (EXTENDED)

**Prepared by:** PC 3341 YATES  
**Date:** 22 October 2024 (revised 01/11/2024)  
**Case ref:** CB-2024-BIR-03189

## Full summary of case

At approximately 01:15 on 20 October 2024, a fire was reported at 2–4 Canal Wharf, Digbeth, Birmingham. The premises comprise a small yard and a single-storey commercial storage unit used by Daniel REECE for storage of tools and materials. The fire service attended. The unit was severely damaged by fire; the cause was determined as deliberate ignition. There was no evidence of accidental cause (electrical, heating, or natural).

The prosecution case is that the defendant Marcus David WEBB started the fire. A neighbour (Fiona MERRITT) states that at about 01:00 she was woken by noise and saw a male in dark clothing in the yard; she saw a flickering light and shortly afterwards smoke. She described the male as of medium build, wearing a hooded top. She could not see his face. The defendant was identified subsequently by other means: his vehicle was captured on ANPR in the vicinity at 00:52 and 01:18. The defendant had a dispute with the owner Daniel REECE following a failed business deal; REECE had obtained a county court judgment against the defendant in August 2024. The defendant was arrested at his home address on 21 October 2024. He made no comment in interview after legal advice.

Forensic work is ongoing. A fire cause report from the fire investigation team is awaited. Scene examination has identified an area of origin near the rear door of the unit; accelerant cannot be ruled out but has not yet been confirmed. No CCTV covers the interior of the yard; a neighbouring business had external CCTV but it was overwritten before a request for preservation was made. The prosecution will rely on motive (the dispute and CCJ), opportunity (ANPR and neighbour’s description of timing and location), and the absence of any other credible cause for the fire.

Defence have not yet served a defence statement. The defendant has put forward no account. The prosecution accept that identification is not by direct recognition (no witness who knows the defendant saw him at the scene) but by circumstantial evidence including ANPR, motive, and exclusion of accident.
