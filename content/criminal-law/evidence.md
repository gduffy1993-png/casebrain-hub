# Evidence – Hearsay, bad character, confessions
Summary reference for key evidence rules in criminal proceedings (England & Wales). See Criminal Justice Act 2003, PACE 1984, and case law.

---

## Hearsay – general rule and admissibility
A statement not made in oral evidence is admissible as evidence of any matter stated only if (a) any provision in the CJA 2003 or another statute provides for admissibility, (b) a common law rule preserved by the Act so provides, or (c) all parties agree. The main statutory gateways include: first-hand hearsay where the maker is unavailable (e.g. dead, unfit, abroad, cannot be found); business and other documents; previous inconsistent statements; multiple hearsay where in the interests of justice. The court may exclude hearsay under the inclusionary discretion (section 114(1)(d)) or the exclusionary discretion (section 126) where the reliability of the evidence is doubtful or its admission would result in unfairness.

---

## Hearsay – capability and credibility
Where hearsay is admitted, the court may draw such inferences as appear proper. The opposing party may attack the credibility of the maker and adduce evidence to that effect. The court must have regard to any risk of unfairness to the defendant and may give a direction or exclude the evidence under section 126.

---

## Bad character – defendant
Evidence of a defendant’s bad character (disposition, previous convictions, other misconduct) is admissible only through one of the gateways in section 101 CJA 2003: (a) all parties agree; (b) adduced by the defendant; (c) important explanatory evidence; (d) relevant to an important matter in issue between defendant and prosecution; (e) substantial probative value in relation to an important matter in issue between defendant and co-defendant; (f) evidence to correct a false impression given by the defendant; (g) defendant has made an attack on another person’s character. The court must exclude under section 101(3) where the probative value is outweighed by the risk of unfairness. Section 78 PACE also applies.

---

## Bad character – non-defendant
Evidence of a non-defendant’s bad character is admissible if it is relevant to an important matter in issue and has substantial probative value, or if it is important explanatory evidence, or if all parties agree. The court must exclude where the probative value is outweighed by the risk of unfairness.

---

## Confessions – admissibility
A confession may be given in evidence in so far as it is relevant and not excluded. A confession is excluded if it was obtained by oppression or in consequence of anything said or done which was likely to render it unreliable (section 76(2) PACE). The prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the confession was not so obtained. A confession may also be excluded under section 78 PACE (unfairness). A confession by one defendant may be used against a co-defendant only in limited circumstances (section 76A PACE).

---

## Confessions – oppression and unreliability
Oppression includes torture, inhuman or degrading treatment, and the use or threat of violence. Unreliability may arise from things said or done (e.g. inducements, lack of appropriate adult, breach of Code C). The court must consider the circumstances in which the confession was made. If the defence raises the issue, the judge must hold a voir dire (trial within a trial) unless the prosecution does not seek to rely on the confession.

---

## Silence and adverse inferences
Under the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994, the court or jury may draw such inferences as appear proper from the defendant’s failure to mention facts when questioned under caution (or when charged) which he or she later relies on; failure to give evidence at trial; or failure to account for objects, substances or marks, or presence at a place. The suspect must have been cautioned and (where applicable) have had the opportunity to consult a solicitor. No inference may be drawn from silence alone; there must be a case to answer. The judge must direct the jury appropriately.
