# Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996
Source: legislation.gov.uk – Crown copyright. Key provisions for disclosure.

---

## Part I – Disclosure

### Section 1 – Application of Part I
This Part applies where a person is charged with a summary offence or an either-way offence that is to be tried summarily, or with an indictable offence.

### Section 2 – General interpretation
(1) For the purposes of this Part, material is “sensitive” if its disclosure would have a real risk of serious prejudice to an important public interest.
(2) “Prosecution material” means material which is in the prosecutor’s possession and came into his possession in connection with the case for the prosecution against the accused.

### Section 3 – Primary disclosure by prosecutor
(1) The prosecutor must disclose to the accused any prosecution material which has not previously been disclosed to the accused and which might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution against the accused or of assisting the case for the accused.
(2) For the purposes of this section, prosecution material is material which is in the prosecutor’s possession and came into his possession in connection with the case for the prosecution against the accused.
(3) Where the prosecutor has material to which subsection (1) applies, he must disclose it to the accused as soon as is reasonably practicable after the accused has given a defence statement or, if no defence statement has been given, after the relevant time (subject to any order under section 8).

### Section 5 – Compulsory disclosure by accused
(1) Subject to subsections (2) to (4), this section applies where the accused gives a defence statement.
(2) Where the accused gives a defence statement and the case is to be tried in the Crown Court, the accused must give the prosecutor and the court a defence statement.
(3) Where the accused gives a defence statement and the case is to be tried in the magistrates’ court, the accused must give the prosecutor a defence statement.

### Section 6 – Voluntary disclosure by accused
(1) The accused may give the prosecutor a defence statement at any time after the prosecutor has complied or purported to comply with section 3.
(2) If the accused gives a defence statement, he must give it to the prosecutor and, if the case is to be tried in the Crown Court, to the court.

### Section 7 – Defence statement
(1) A defence statement is a written statement setting out in general terms the nature of the accused’s defence and indicating the matters on which he takes issue with the prosecution.
(2) A defence statement must indicate the matters of fact on which the accused takes issue with the prosecution and, in respect of each, the reason why he takes issue.
(3) A defence statement must set out particulars of any alibi and the name and address of any witness the accused believes is able to give evidence in support of the alibi.

### Section 8 – Application by accused for disclosure
(1) The accused may apply to the court for an order that the prosecutor disclose material which might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution or of assisting the case for the accused.
(2) On an application under subsection (1), the court may order the prosecutor to disclose any material which might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution or of assisting the case for the accused.
(3) The court may only make an order under subsection (2) if it is satisfied that the prosecutor has not disclosed material that might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution or of assisting the case for the accused.

### Section 9 – Continuing duty of prosecutor to disclose
(1) The prosecutor must keep under review the question whether there is any prosecution material which might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution or of assisting the case for the accused and which has not been disclosed to the accused.
(2) If at any time there is any such material, the prosecutor must disclose it to the accused as soon as is reasonably practicable.

### Section 10 – Prosecutor’s failure to disclose
(1) This section applies where the prosecutor has failed to comply with section 3 or 9 or with an order under section 8.
(2) The court may make such order as it thinks fit to secure the disclosure of the material in question.

### Section 12 – Public interest: review
(1) The prosecutor must keep under review the question whether it is in the public interest to disclose any material that would otherwise fall to be disclosed under section 3 or 9.
(2) Material must not be disclosed under section 3 or 9 to the extent that the court, on an application by the prosecutor, concludes that it is in the public interest not to disclose it.

### Section 14 – Unused material
(1) “Unused material” means material which is in the prosecutor’s possession and which has not been used as evidence in the case for the prosecution.
(2) The prosecutor’s duty of disclosure extends to unused material that might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution or of assisting the case for the accused.

## Part II – Defence disclosure

### Section 22 – Time for defence disclosure
(1) The accused must give a defence statement within such period as may be prescribed by rules of court.
(2) If the accused fails to give a defence statement within that period, the court may draw such inferences as appear proper.

### Section 23 – Contents of defence statement
A defence statement must set out the nature of the defence in general terms and indicate the matters on which the accused takes issue with the prosecution.

## Reasonable lines of enquiry
The prosecutor and the investigator must pursue all reasonable lines of enquiry, whether they point towards or away from the accused. Material that is relevant to a reasonable line of enquiry must be recorded and retained, and disclosed if it meets the test in section 3.

## Disclosure officer
The disclosure officer is responsible for examining material retained by the investigator, recording relevant material, and preparing schedules of non-sensitive and sensitive material for the prosecutor. The prosecutor relies on the disclosure officer to ensure that all relevant material is identified and disclosed in accordance with the Act.

## Abuse of process
Persistent or serious failure by the prosecution to comply with disclosure duties may found an application to stay the proceedings as an abuse of process. The court will consider whether the accused can receive a fair trial.
