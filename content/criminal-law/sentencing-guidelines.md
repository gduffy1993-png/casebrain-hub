# Sentencing guidelines – Summary reference
Summary of the sentencing framework in England & Wales. Definitive guidelines are issued by the Sentencing Council. See sentencingcouncil.org.uk and legislation.gov.uk.

---

## Role of the Sentencing Council
The Sentencing Council produces definitive guidelines for sentencing in the magistrates’ court and Crown Court. Guidelines are intended to promote consistency while allowing courts to take account of the particular facts of the case. Courts must follow guidelines unless it would be contrary to the interests of justice to do so (Coroners and Justice Act 2009).

---

## Determining offence seriousness
Seriousness is determined by the culpability of the offender and the harm caused or intended. Guidelines set out categories of culpability (e.g. intention, recklessness, negligence) and harm (e.g. physical, financial, psychological). Starting points and ranges are given for each category. Aggravating and mitigating factors may move the sentence within or between ranges.

---

## Guilty plea reduction
A reduction in sentence for a guilty plea is applied in accordance with the Sentencing Council guideline. The maximum reduction is one-third for a plea at the first stage; the reduction decreases the later the plea. The court must give credit for a guilty plea and state the extent of the reduction.

---

## Totality and consecutive sentences
Where an offender is sentenced for more than one offence, or is already serving a sentence, the court must consider totality so that the total sentence is just and proportionate. Consecutive sentences may be appropriate where offences are unrelated; concurrent where they arise from the same incident or course of conduct.

---

## Custodial sentences
A custodial sentence may be imposed only where the offence is so serious that neither a fine nor a community order is sufficient. The court must consider a pre-sentence report (PSR) in certain cases (e.g. first-time custody, under 18). The length of the custodial term must be the shortest that is commensurate with seriousness. For determinate sentences, the offender will serve half in custody (subject to release on licence) unless the offence is one specified for extended licence or the sentence is extended.

---

## Community orders and fines
Community orders may include requirements such as unpaid work, rehabilitation, curfew, or prohibited activity. The court must be satisfied that the requirements are suitable and that restriction on liberty is commensurate with seriousness. Fines are calculated by reference to the offender’s means (income or equivalent) and the seriousness of the offence. The court must give reasons where it does not follow a guideline.

---

## Mitigation and personal mitigation
Personal mitigation (e.g. good character, remorse, ill health, caring responsibilities) may reduce the sentence. The court must take into account any mitigation advanced. Reduction for assistance to the prosecution is governed by separate guidelines and may be substantial.
