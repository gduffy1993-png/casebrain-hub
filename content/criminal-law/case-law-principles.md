# Case law principles – Criminal law (England & Wales)
Summary reference to established principles from appellate case law. Not a substitute for full law reports.

---

## Abuse of process and stay
The court has an inherent power to stay proceedings as an abuse of process where to continue would be unfair or would bring the administration of justice into disrepute. Examples include: delay (where the delay is unjustifiable and has caused serious prejudice); entrapment; loss of evidence; breach of promise not to prosecute; manipulation of process. The threshold is high; a stay is exceptional. The judge must balance the interests of the defendant, the victim, and the public.

---

## Disclosure and fairness
The prosecution’s duty of disclosure is ongoing. Material that might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the prosecution case or of assisting the defence must be disclosed. Failure to disclose may result in a stay, exclusion of evidence, or quashing of a conviction on appeal (e.g. R v H and C; R v McLeod). The defence must serve a defence statement to trigger the continuing duty and to define the issues. Applications for disclosure may be made to the court under section 8 CPIA.

---

## Identification evidence
Turnbull guidelines apply where the case depends wholly or substantially on visual identification. The judge must direct the jury on the need for caution and on the factors that affect reliability (e.g. visibility, distance, length of observation, discrepancy with description). Where the quality of the identification evidence is poor, the case should be withdrawn from the jury unless there is other evidence that supports the identification. Recognition (e.g. of someone known) may be more reliable but still requires a careful direction.

---

## Joint enterprise and secondary liability
A person may be guilty as a secondary party (aider, abettor, counsellor, procurer) or under joint enterprise principles. For secondary participation, the prosecution must prove assistance or encouragement and the requisite intent. For joint enterprise (common purpose), the prosecution must prove that the defendant participated in a crime with a common purpose and foresaw that the principal might commit the act (e.g. murder) with the necessary intent. The law was restated in R v Jogee; the Supreme Court held that the secondary party’s mental element is intention to assist or encourage the principal’s act, with foresight of that act as evidence of intent.

---

## Self-defence and prevention of crime
A person may use such force as is reasonable in the circumstances in the prevention of crime or in self-defence (or defence of another) (Criminal Law Act 1967, common law). The jury must consider whether the defendant believed (even if mistakenly) that force was necessary and whether the force used was reasonable in the circumstances as the defendant believed them to be. The defendant does not have to wait to be attacked; pre-emptive force may be justified.

---

## Intoxication and mens rea
Voluntary intoxication is generally no defence to a crime of basic intent (e.g. assault). It may be relevant to specific intent (e.g. murder, where the jury may consider whether the defendant formed the intent). Involuntary intoxication may negative mens rea. The distinction between basic and specific intent and the application of intoxication were considered in R v Majewski and subsequent cases.
