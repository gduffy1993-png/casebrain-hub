# Criminal procedure – Allocation, bail, case management
Summary reference for magistrates’ court and Crown Court procedure (England & Wales). See Magistrates’ Courts Act 1980, Bail Act 1976, Criminal Procedure Rules, and practice directions.

---

## Allocation (either-way offences)
For either-way offences, the defendant is asked to indicate a plea (guilty, not guilty, or no indication). If the defendant indicates a guilty plea, the court may sentence in the magistrates’ court or commit for sentence to the Crown Court. If the defendant indicates not guilty or gives no indication, the court decides whether the case is suitable for summary trial. The court must have regard to the nature of the case, the adequacy of its sentencing powers, and any representations. If the court accepts jurisdiction, the defendant may then choose trial at the Crown Court. If the court declines jurisdiction or the defendant elects Crown Court trial, the case is sent to the Crown Court. For indictable-only offences, the case is sent to the Crown Court without allocation.

---

## Bail – general principle and conditions
There is a general right to bail (Bail Act 1976). Bail may be refused only where one or more of the statutory exceptions apply (e.g. risk of absconding, further offences, interference with witnesses, need for own protection). The court may impose conditions (e.g. residence, curfew, surety, reporting). Conditions must be necessary and proportionate. The defendant may apply to vary conditions. Breach of bail may lead to arrest and remand.

---

## Bail – remand in custody
Where the court refuses bail, it must give reasons. Remand in custody may be for no more than 8 clear days at a time (unless the defendant is already serving a sentence or the court has received a pre-sentence report or other information it has requested). For indictable-only or either-way offences sent to the Crown Court, the magistrates may remand for up to 28 days. The defendant must be produced at each remand hearing unless the court directs otherwise (e.g. by live link).

---

## Case management – magistrates’ court
The Criminal Procedure Rules require the court and the parties to manage cases actively. At the first hearing the court will identify the issues, give directions (e.g. disclosure, defence statement, listing), and fix a trial date. The defendant must be asked to indicate a plea at an early stage. Case progression hearings ensure readiness for trial. Failure to comply with rules or directions may lead to sanctions (e.g. adverse inference, cost orders).

---

## Case management – Crown Court
In the Crown Court, the court will give directions at the plea and trial preparation hearing (PTPH) and at subsequent hearings. The parties must complete the case management form and comply with disclosure obligations. Defence statements must be served in accordance with the CPIA. The court will fix a trial date and ensure that disclosure, witness availability, and legal aid are in place. Pre-trial reviews may be held to resolve issues before trial.

---

## Disclosure and defence statements
The prosecution’s duty of disclosure (CPIA 1996) and the defence’s duty to serve a defence statement are central to case management. The defence statement must set out the nature of the defence and the matters in issue. Failure to serve a defence statement or to mention a matter later relied on may allow the court or jury to draw adverse inferences. The court may review disclosure and case management at a hearing and give further directions.
